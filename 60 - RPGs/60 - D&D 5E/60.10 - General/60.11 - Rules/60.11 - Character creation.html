dnd

  • A character is a combination of statistics, information, and imagination
  • All characters must have a race, a class, and ability scores
    • Races contribute to identify and have certain mechanical benefits
    • Classes broadly describe what tactics a character might employ, and also gives mechanical benefits
    • Ability scores have various uses for throws and character statistics
  • There are 6 ability scores
    • Strength:
      • This is how easily you can crush a tomato
      • It measures athleticism and power
      • It is useful for barbarians, fighters, and paladins
    • Dexterity:
      • This is how easily you can dodge a tomato
      • It measures agility, reflexes, balance, and poise
      • It is useful for monks, rangers, and rogues
    • Constitution:
      • This is how easily you can eat a rotten tomato
      • It measures health, stamina, and vitality
      • It is useful for everyone
    • Intelligence:
      • This is knowing that a tomato is a fruit
      • It measures mental acuity, information recall, and analytical skill
      • It is useful for wizards
    • Wisdom:
      • This is knowing that a tomato wouldn't work in a fruit salad
      • It measures awareness, intuition, and insight
      • It is useful for clerics and druids
    • Charisma:
      • This is being able to sell a fruit salad with tomatoes
      • It measures confidence, eloquence, and leadership
      • It is useful for bards, sorcerers, and warlocks

  • Ability scores can be determined by a standard array or randomly with dice
    • The standard array is the following: 15, 14, 13, 12, 10, 8
    • To roll it randomly, roll a 4d6, pick the top 3 numbers, and note down the sum
      • Once you've done this 6 times, place the scores where you want them
      • You are allowed to reroll once
  • A proficiency bonus is a bonus that is added only to checks that you are proficient in
    • Your class and background gives you some proficiencies
  • Your class gives you a certain hit die, which sets your initial health to the maximum of your hit die plus your constitution modifier
    • After resting, you can spend a hit die to recover hit points
    • Every time you gain a level, you gain an extra hit die
      • Roll the hit die, add your constitution modifier to it, and add the total to your hit point maximum
  • Armour class represents how you can avoid damage
    • Armour and shields are common ways to raise AC
    • Without armour or a shield, your AC is equal to 10 + your dexterity modifier
  • When you use a weapon you are proficient in, you roll a d20 and add your proficiency bonus
    • A melee attack generally uses your strength modifier, with dexterity for finesse weapons
    • A ranged attack generally uses your dexterity modifier, with strength for thrown weapons
  • Once you reach a specific XP threshold, you gain a level
