Introduction

War has long been glorified, through manipulation of a sense of patriotism and honour and by causing young men to feel guilty for not serving their country via enlisting in the army. Even now, the military entices young men into signing up and going to a faraway country, simply to kill others. Like flies to honey, with potential soldiers the flies, and the presumed benefits the honey, the military sweetens the pot with promises of discharged student loans, education benefits, and cash bonuses, while placing guilt upon those who don't enlist. This is shown both in the poem Disabled by Wilfred Owen, written during World War I and published after his death, and through the notorious Lord Kitchener Wants You poster, created in World War I and spawning countless imitations.

Disabled

Disabled is a poem by the English poet Wilfred Owen. It is about the aftermath of World War I, and how veterans were treated when they came back from the war. In Disabled, it is clear that at least part of the poem is about the various things done to convince men to enlist. For example, the lines "He thought of jewelled hilts / For daggers in plaid socks" shows that the soldiers anticipated glory and rewards when they joined, further emphasized by jewelled, since jewels are expensive, but in reality didn't truly know what to expect. Alongside that, later in the poem it is said that "he was drafted out with drums and cheers". This shows that even up to the moment before they were drafted, the illusion of grandeur was being cast upon the soldiers, fooling them up until it was too late for them. It is obvious that these lines show the glorification of war during World War I negatively, especially since Disabled was intended to show the dark side of the war.

Lord Kitchener Wants You

Lord Kitchener Wants You is an iconic poster, also from World War I. It was a recruitment poster that was intended to encourage young men to join the army. It depicted the British Secretary of State for War, Lord Kitchener, pointing at you, with text below saying "Wants you", and in red, "Join your country's army! / God save the king". Usage of "your" makes the person reading the poster feel that since the country they are in is "theirs", they are obligated to protect it. Additionally, the poster was designed specifically so that the eyes and finger seem to follow the viewer, no matter their orientation relative to the poster. From this, it is reasonable to assume that it was designed to cause guilt in people, leading to them enlisting in the army.

Conclusion

Throughout the two examples, it is evident that they display how war was glorified. Disabled casts this whole process in a negative light, however, the Lord Kitchener Wants You poster casts war in a positive light, in order to persuade citizens into joining the army. From these examples, it is logical to view glorification of war as a global issue, since the victims potentially don't realize that they are victims until it is far too late.
